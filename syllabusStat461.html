This course seeks to prepare you to have a solid foundation of the core ideas in the Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) toolbox. To this end, I anticipate that we will cover the following concepts:

Review Concepts

Exploratory Data Analysis vs. Confirmatory Data Analysis, Randomization, Descriptive (Incisive) Statistics (e.g., sample arithmetic mean, sample arithmetic variance, sums of squares, degrees of freedom, etc.), Methods of Statistical Inference (Replication vs. Simulation, Bootstrapping, Permutation, Monte Carlo A & B, Shortcuts-Parametric, Nonparametric)

New Concepts

Experimental Design, Types of Units, Factors and their levels, Hasse Diagrams, Model Building, Oneway ANOVA (Assumptions, Visualizations, Post Hoc, Effect Size, Writing Up Results), Model Remediation (Nonparametrics, Transformations, Box-Cox), Blocking, Twoway ANOVA (Interaction Terms), k-Way ANOVA, Fixed and & Random Effects, More Advanced Designs (Analysis of Covariance-ANCOVA, Repeated Measures, Nested, Response Surface, Split-plot).

Course Goals, Objectives, and Learning Outcomes

The Stat 461-Analysis of Variance course provides students with the foundational skills, understandings, and ways of thinking so that they can meaningfully address one-way and k-way data analysis problems and research questions.

  1. Students will develop their statistical literacy, reasoning, and thinking within the context of ANOVA problems.
  2. Students will develop their communication skills alongside their statistical understandings so that they can present their work to peers, superiors, and clients in effective ways.
  3. Students will develop their skill with at least one statistical software package so that they can use technology to handle computations, allowing them to focus on designs, interpretations, and communication.
  4. Students will develop their knowledge and understandings of ANOVA models and their applicability to real-world statistical research questions.
  5. Students will develop their skills in data analysis to describe what the data have to say and explain what could be going on in a particular context.
  6. Students will develop their skills in using the tools of statistical inference to make decisions.

Self-plagiarism occurs whenever a person re-uses old work with minor revisions. Group work occurs when the instructor gives an activity or assignment that is meant to be completed by a group of students together and submit a single write up with everyone's names.

Each question on homework, quiz, test, and the final exam will be tied to at least one of the learning outcomes. Your score on that question will be score as one of the following:

  • Full Credit: your response is consistent with the target ways of thinking. Your response might not be worded in the same way as what's in the answer key, but the conveyed meaning is productive. You get 100% of the points for that question.
  • Advanced: your response is nearly consistent with the answer key. There might be 1-2 minor issues that you need to work on. You get 85% of the points for that question.
  • Proficient: your response has at least half of the elements of the answer key but there are elements that conflict with the answer key. These discrepancies are minor or moderate. You get 70% of the points for that question.
  • (Not Yet) Progressing: your response has some elements (less than half) of the answer key but there are multiple discrepancies for you to address. A single major discrepancy (e.g., circular reasoning) would put your response in this category. You get 50% of the points for that question.
  • (Not Yet) Beginning: your response speaks to the prompt but there are multiple major discrepancies and/or the response is incomplete (e.g., you started answering the question but did not finish your response). You get 25% of the points for that question.
  • Not Shown (Off Topic/Can't Follow/Didn't Answer): Any of the following:
    • your response does not reasonably address the question posed (E.g., stated true facts about probability when asked to interpret a probability value)
    • I can’t reasonably follow the your response.
    • You did not attempt a response (e.g., blank answers)
    You receive 0% of the points for that question.

Throughout the semester, your work from all homework, quizzes, tests, and the data analysis project connects to the various learning outcomes associated with this course. The learning outcomes allow for you to demonstrate your skills and understanding over the whole semester rather than a “high stakes” exams. For each learning outcome, your scores on associated questions helps determine your status on that learning outcome. There are five statuses:

  • Minor
    • Grammar issues that interfere with interpreting your response
    • Missing a non-critical part of solution
    • Calculational error that the grader can track (e.g., a +/- error)
    • Using lower rather than upper case for a stochastic variable
    • Using the wrong unit (e.g., problems says data units are inches, student uses centimeters)
    • Tests Only: using the word “it” (provided we can reasonably still make sense of the response)
  • Moderate
    • Missing several parts of the prompt
    • Applying the wrong unit to a statistic’s value (e.g., not making the units on values of the SAM reflect the rate nature)
    • Using the wrong notation (e.g., Probability Notation, \(P\left[X\leq5|X\sim\mathcal{N}\left(0,1\right)\right]\), instead Distribution Function Notation, \(C_{N}\left(5|0,1\right)\) )
    • Tests Only: using the word “average” outside of the phrases “average rate of change” and “l’homme moyen”
  • Major
    • Restating the prompt as the response (e.g., Prompt: Explain what a probability value of 0.2 means; Response: That the probability of something happening is 20%.)
    • Only listing the attribute when asked to define a stochastic variable.
    • Circular Reasoning (e.g., probability is chance; probability is how likely)
    • Giving a response consistent with specifically identified problematic meanings in class (red text in slides) (e.g., distribution as a picture, distribution as a listing of what values and how often they occur, interpreting confidence intervals with the Fundamental Confidence Fallacy)
    • Other conceptual issues

These are examples to help you get a sense of the potential issues.
